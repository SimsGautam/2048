MIT 6.470 Web Development

      During the Independent Activities Period (IAP) at MIT, students have the month of January to work on any project that they want. I used this time to get my feet wet in web development by creating a database-backed website with dynamically generated pages. Working in a team with three other students, we decided to make a website that helps local musicians connect with local fans as an event-finding and social network application.

      My role in the team involved backend development with Ruby on Rails and some front-end design. From this project, I gained proficiency in HTML, CSS, Ruby on Rails, and some exposure to Javascript. Additionally, our website, Metronotes, was awarded second place among 40 teams at the MIT 6.470 Web Development competition for functionality, aesthetics, and viability.

Custom Built Automous Sailboat

      My ‘senior project’ in high school was an ambitious, 6-month undertaking to build a fully autonomous, 1-meter sailboat from scratch with four other students. In these six months, we acquired several thousand dollars in funding from local companies, designed and 3D-printed some parts of the boat, and built a control system around an Arduino 2560 microcontroller using an integrated GPS and wind sensors. Lastly, we programmed a waypoint navigation algorithm that gave our sailboat the autonomy to sail by herself.

      My role in the project was developing the waypoint navigation algorithm in C, contributing to the hull design on Autodesk, and communicating with dozens of tech companies to fund our project. From this project, I also gained a lot of experience in team collaboration and figuring out quick fixes to small problems that popped up throughout the project.

      In June of 2013, we went to Gloucester, MA to compete in the International Robotic Sailing Regatta. There, we were awarded first place in North America in the one-meter class after our team won all the autonomous events and the Innovation award for her six segment 3D-printed hull.

Spherical Codes Problem

      I spent the summer after my junior year at doing research with the MIT Math Department as a participant in the Research Science Institute. Under the mentorship of a graduate student, I started my research on the Spherical Codes Problem, an open problem in discrete geometry and optimization, that summer.

       After the summer ended, I continued working on this problem in between classes during the school year. By the end, I created a gradient descent algorithm to optimally distribute points in higher dimensional spheres. This algorithm utilized concepts from group theory and produced some novel results for the problem.

       I received several accolades for this project, including Second Place in Mathematics at the 2013 Intel International Science and Engineering Fair, Third Place Karl Menger Memorial Award by the American Mathematical Society, and "Best of Show" at the 2013 National Junior Science and Humanities Symposium.

Bitcoin Research

       To keep myself busy during my sophomore year of high school, I started looking into Bitcoin, which, at the time, was only one year old and not very well known. I spent several months with a friend understanding Bitcoins in great detail and focused on documenting its vulerabilities. Eventually, we wrote a 36-page paper titled "On the Vulnerability of the Bitcoin Distributed Electronic Cash System".

       One thing we focused on in this paper was the feasibility of a 51% attack, where the attacker amasses more CPU power than the rest of the network. We mined data from Bitcoin IRC Bot and estimated that, at the time, an attacker could amass more CPU power than the network for $136,000 per hour. This estimate was calculated assuming the attacker uses Amazon's Cluster GPU Instances.

      We chose not to publicly distribute our research, but email me if you're interested in reading the paper. Most of the statistics in the paper are now outdated.

Primitive Root Densities

      The summer after my freshman year in high school, I went to Boston University to study number theory at PROMYS. After coming back from this program, I decided to independently pursue an open-ended problem in number theory regarding full-reptend primes. I extensively studied the densities and distributions of these primes in different bases.

       After several months of exploring and thinking, I came up with several conjectures of my own and proved them rigorously. I presented my work at the 2011 Intel ISEF, where I received Second Place Grand Award in Mathematics and recognition from MIT Lincoln Lab by having a minor planet named after me.
